Interesting reads: "Los Maestros del Hombre" (Revista Mundo Desconocido, 1978)

La antigua civilización maya mantenía una perspectiva única del mundo que se entrelazaba con diversos aspectos de su cultura y espiritualidad. Uno de estos aspectos era su percepción de la Tierra, que consideraban como cuadrada, enfatizando la importancia de los puntos cardinales. Esta creencia se refleja en el concepto de los "Cuatro Bacabes," representados por cuatro figuras mitológicas: Kan Bacab (Este), Bacab (Rojo, en el Este), Ek Chuah (Norte) y Cab (Negro, en el Oeste). Estos puntos cardinales desempeñaban un papel vital en la cosmología maya. Uno de los símbolos centrales en la cultura maya era el Chac-Mool, que representaba la dualidad de la naturaleza, la unión de géneros y la fertilidad del mundo. Este símbolo tenía importancia universal en diferentes civilizaciones antiguas y sigue siendo relevante en la actualidad. Incluso se sugiere que Jesús podría haber tenido un profundo conocimiento de la religión maya, tras haber pasado tiempo en el Himalaya estudiando diversas creencias. Esto plantea preguntas intrigantes sobre la influencia potencial de la espiritualidad maya en sus enseñanzas. Además, la civilización maya parece tener conexiones con otras sociedades antiguas como Egipto y Babilonia. Se propone que los mayas transmitieron sus conocimientos a Egipto, influyendo en la cultura y tradiciones egipcias. La idea de que los primeros acadios eran descendientes de personas Naga-Maya que migraron desde la India a través del Golfo Pérsico y se establecieron en lo que ahora conocemos como Akkad (que significa "suelo suave") sugiere un origen compartido. Esta conexión entre los mayas y otras civilizaciones antiguas cuenta con el respaldo de autoridades académicas. Las enseñanzas originales del Príncipe Gautama, también conocido como Buda, estaban arraigadas en la religión maya. Sin embargo, algunos sacerdotes budistas en el norte de la India se apartaron de estas enseñanzas, introduciendo conceptos teológicos. Vale la pena mencionar que solo los sacerdotes del sur permanecieron fieles a las enseñanzas originales de Gautama. La palabra sagrada "AUM" se cita como ejemplo de la influencia maya en los textos budistas, demostrando cómo la espiritualidad maya podría haber dejado su huella en otros sistemas de creencias. El náhuatl, símbolo central en la simbología maya, se describe como un símbolo de movimiento y se equipara con el Nahui Ollin en náhuatl, que representa el concepto de movimiento en la cosmología maya. Además, se aborda el triángulo sobre un cuadrado, que simboliza la divinidad sobre la Tierra, formando una cruz con los rayos solares. La suma de tres en el triángulo y cuatro en el cuadrado da como resultado el número sagrado siete, que tiene importancia en numerosas religiones antiguas. Por último, el texto hace referencia a la corrupción de la religión en el antiguo Egipto durante la XVIII Dinastía, atribuyéndola a la invención de espíritus amenazantes por parte de los sacerdotes egipcios. Moisés se presenta como una figura que poseía conocimiento de la cultura maya, aunque sus escritos fueron distorsionados con el tiempo.

Texto original

"Los Maestros del Hombre" por Ignacio Magaloni Duarte, Revista Mundo Desconocido, Edición Nº19 Enero de 1978

Local food week challenge day Nº7 (final day): Milanesa a la napolitana

The base of this recipe (milanesa), more commonly known worldwide by it's German version "Schnitzel", is one of those recipes that's so deeply ingrained in our culture we tend to forget how widespread this recipe is so widespread, especially in Europe where I recently learned about this recipe being a staple of literally every country and beyond. Our version comes from Italy originally, where it's called "cotoletta", although it has mutated into something slightly different locally through the years, especially in the variation I am cooking today: Milanesa a la napolitana.
A dish with a similar name exists in Italy (Cotoletta alla milanese), but it doesn't share the same origin and is nothing alike. What we know as "milanesa a la napolitana" here is essentially just our standard beef milanesa, but with tomato sauce and Mozzarella, essentially a Schnitzel pizza if you will.
I have made french fries as a side dish which is very common here.
  • Thin beef cut (similar to what I used on day Nº1 for Chivito
  • Bread crumbs
  • Egg (beaten)
  • Tomato sauce
  • Mozzarella

Local food week challenge day Nº6: Asado

"Every creature that walks, ends up on the grill" Goes the popular saying, and it's very true in Uruguay. We are big meat eaters here (especially beef), and therefore no dish is considered complete if it doesn't have meat in some shape of form. If you look at all the dishes I've been doing for this challenge, every single one is centered around meat excepto for "cappelletti a la Caruso" and even then, the filling for the pasta was beef.
My parents are vegetarian and I was raised as such. However, unlike my siblings who are vegetarian to this day, I couldn't keep my draconic spirit at bay and since I was a little kid I would take any chance I had to eat meat and as an adult it has become part of my daily diet, much like my fellow countryman's.
Asado literally means "grilled" and it's essentially the regional version of probably one of the most widespread customs throughout the world, which is the primitive act of cooking meat over a fire. There's a twist however: We don't really cook under a fire, but rather make a fire apart from the grill and then as hot coals are made, we pull them under the grill. This cooking method means the meat can be cooked really slowly, which allows for tougher cuts of meats to be tenderized. Regardless of the cut, the local preference for meat is almost always well done except for the few of us who are a bit "eccentric" and prefer it "BIEN jugoso" which for an American would be just medium, not even medium rare.
Asado is a very popular festive food and it's typically eaten for larger groups like a family get together, birthday parties, Christmas, etc. There's a lot of variety when it comes to options for grilling: Many cuts of beef, pork, lamb, chicken and even fish can be part of asado, but it doesn't end with meat. There's also chorizo, blood sausage, lots of organ meat, corn, potatoes, sweet potatoes, and many more.
That said, considering I'm cooking for myself, I had to keep it simple in order not to waste food:
  • Asado (rib cut)
  • Chorizo
  • Morcilla dulce (sweet blood sausage, with raisins & peanuts)
  • Chinchulines (cow intestine)
  • Mollejas (cow thymus gland)

Local food week challenge day Nº5: Chicken empanadas

Empanadas are among the most recognizable dishes of this region. A popular fast food item, it can be found throughout South America and due to immigration it started spreading to places like New York, USA. It originates in Spain, like much of our cuisine, and every country (and sometimes regions within a country like the case of Argentina) has their own variations on stuffing and sometimes even the dough itself, which is usually made with wheat flour but some countries like Venezuela use corn flour.
Here in Uruguay the typical empanada filling is made with beef, but there are other options such as ham & cheese, caprese, calabresa and of course: Chicken.

  • Empanada dough disks
  • Ground chicken meat
  • Adobo (local spice mix)
  • Tomato sauce
  • Onions
  • Garlic
  • Hardboiled egg
  • Olives

Local food week challenge day Nº4: Choripán

For the 4th day I have decided to make yet another typical sandwich of this region, and one that's enjoyed in neighboring countries as well: The choripán.
Essentially a chorizo sandwich, it's an extremely popular fast food option and can be found essentially everywhere, in foodtrucks or even simple portable grills that sellers set in the street. The unmistakable aroma can be smelled in any festival, sports event, party, etc. It's so ubiquitous as a fast food item that it has become a symbol of this region.
Traditionally, it's served in a baguette like bread with more generic dressings such as mayo, mustard, ketchup, etc. and many time with more typical sauces from the region like "salsa criolla" and "chimichurri".
I have decided to skip the traditional sauces for convenience and went for a simpler version that is nonetheless enjoyed, especially in the aforementioned portable grills in which "chefs" prefer to keep things simple:
  • Baguette bread
  • Grilled chorizo (in half)
  • Lettuce
  • Tomato
  • Mozzarella
  • Mayonnaise
  • "Catalán" peppers (the only remotely spicy food eaten traditionally in Uruguay)

Local food week challenge day Nº3: Capelletti a la Caruso

Due to the massive Italian immigration we received from the late 19th century through the 1950s, pasta has become a staple food in Uruguay. From simple everyday dishes like spaghetti with tomato sauce to more complex recipes in the form of filled pasta with more elaborated recipes. We have everything here: all the variations of dried pasta, canelloni, lasagna, agnolotti, ravioli, gnocchi and of course: cappelleti.
Cappelleti is the base of this local dish, but the star is the sauce: Salsa Caruso
The famous Italian tenor, Enrico Caruso, was very popular in the region and he visited Uruguay a handful of times in the early 20th century, leaving his mark in our society. As such, in the 1950s a chef of italian origin named Raymundo Monti created a new sauce in honor of the tenor: the Caruso sauce.
  • Beef cappelletti
  • Ham
  • Beef bouillon
  • Onions
  • Garlic
  • Milk cream
  • Canned sliced button mushrooms
  • Sbrinz cheese
(Not for the faint of the heart! The sauce is literally a roux base but made with literal milk cream as the only liquid. A serving can have up to 200g of it.)

Local food week challenge day Nº2: Uruguayan style guiso

I was doubting about making this one. First and foremost because it's summer here in the southern hemisphere and such a hearty dish feels completely out of place. Secondly, there are literally dozens of variations of stews eaten locally and in an of itself, a stew is just an ubiquitous dish all around the globe, ours being very similar to Spanish and Italian stews. Local food week challenge day Nº1: Chivito

To start this challenge I've chosen our national dish: Chivito
Contrary to what the name would suggest ("chivito" means "goat" in Spanish), chivito is actually a beef sandwich. The story goes that in 1946, an Argentinean woman visited a restaurant in the city of Punta del Este called "El Mejillón Bar" and in a hurry ordered an actual goat meat sandwich, but the chef didn't have any available so he used beef instead. It was then added to the menu and so Chivito was born.
It has been a staple of Uruguayan cuisine ever since and one that people from neighboring countries highly associate with Uruguay. Nowadays it can be found everywhere throughout the country, especially in fast food places and can be eaten "al plato" which is essentially the sandwich ingredients with no bread served over fries, but is more commonly enjoyed in it's sandwich form.

The ingredients may vary from place to place, so I decided to go with the more widespread ones in general but also adding bacon which turns it into a "canadian style" chivito:
  • Baguette bread
  • Tomato
  • Lettuce
  • Hardboiled eggs
  • Ham
  • Bacon
  • Olives
  • Mozzarella
  • Beef steak
  • Pickled gymnopilus spectabilis mushrooms (as far as I know, Uruguay is the only country where these are considered edible)
It's a very simple sandwich, but very tasty and a great option when eating out in Uruguay.
